# Virtual Filmmaking Mastering Autodesk Maya 2013 Chapter 2 Help file [Maya 2014 online Help](http://download.autodesk.com/global/docs/maya2014/en_us/), [Maya tutorial files](http://usa.autodesk.com/adsk/servlet/index?siteID=123112&id=9502844&linkID=9242256) ### The virtual cameras in Maya. >*As with all filmmaking, the story is told through the camera.* Although the technical aspects of using the cameras are not difficult to learn, mastering the art of virtual cinematography can take years of practice. ### Practice in Maya 1. Determine the image size and film speed of the camera - The final size of the rendered image (image sequence) as well as the film speed (frames per second). - Render Settings window - Image size: the number of pixels on the horizontal axis by the number of pixels on the vertical axis. - Resolution: how many pixels fit within an inch. (Computer screen resolution vs. Print resolution) - Image Aspect Ratio: the ratio of width over height. An image that is 720 by 540 has a ratio of 1.333. - Pixel Aspect Ratio: the ratio of the actual pixel size. Computer monitors use square pixels: the height of the pixel is 1, and the width of the pixel is 1; thus, the pixel aspect ratio is 1. Standard video images use nonsquare pixels that are 1 pixel high by 1.1 pixels wide, giving them a pixel aspect ratio of 0.9. - Device Aspect Ratio: the image aspect ratio multiplied by the pixel aspect ratio. For a video image that is 720 by 486 (1.48) using nonsquare pixels (0.9), this would be 1.48 Ã— 0.9 = 1.333. 2. Create and animate cameras - When you add a camera to a scene, you should imagine how the shot will be composed and whether the camera will be animated. > ***Animatics*** > An animatic is a film industry term referring to a rough animation designed to help plan a shot, like a moving storyboard. Typically, models in an animatic are very low resolution and untextured with simple lighting. Animatics are used to plan both computer-generated (CG) and live-action shots. Camera work, timing, and the composition of elements within the frame are the most important aspects of an animatic. - Use depth of field and motion blur ![dof01](img/maya/shading/dof_render.png) ### Depth of Field (DoF) rendering #### Scene preparation 1. Create three plane and five balls on the Cylinder (Create/Polygon Primitives & Edit/Duplicate Special) and align them. 2. Create a camera with a aim (Create/Cameras/Camera and Aim) and check it at the Outliner (two plane mode -> at the left panel or panels/Layouts/Two Panes Side by Side). 3. Choose the camera and transform Snapping the Aim to the main focus point of the object). 4. Adjust the position of camera (panels/Look through Selected) - Choosing Camera properties: (select Camera -> Attribute Editor) - Angle of View, Near and Far clip, Film Gate ![sp03](img/maya/shading/camera.png) 5. Measure the distance between main object to the camera (Create/Measure Tools/Distance Tool) 6. Use the distance for the Focus Distance in the Attribute Editor of the camera - Check Depth of Field (select Camera -> Attribute Editor), change the Focus Distance and F Stop ![sp04](img/maya/shading/dof.png) 7. Render the scene. Do the basic render settings. - Choosing Rendering properties: (Window/Rendering Editors/Render Settings) - Work on Common settings - File name, Image format, Choose camera, Choose Image Size ![sp01](img/maya/shading/render_common.png) - Work on Specific settings -> Maya Software - Quality, Anti-aliasing, Raytracing, Motion Blur ![sp02](img/maya/shading/render_maya.png) ### Inspirational source: [Limbo](http://limbogame.org/media/) ### Review: [Tumblr](http://2014ant3012.tumblr.com/) ___________________________
